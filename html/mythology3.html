The Swallow and the Rice Farmer (Japanese Mythology)

The Kind Farmer and the Injured Swallow

Long ago, in a quiet village surrounded by golden rice fields, lived an old farmer named Gorō. He was a humble man who worked tirelessly in his paddies from sunrise to sunset. Though he had little, he always shared what he had, offering rice to the birds and animals who visited his fields.

One cold autumn morning, as he was threshing rice outside his home, Gorō heard a faint chirping sound. He looked around and saw a small swallow struggling in the grass. Its tiny wing was broken, and it could not fly.

Feeling pity for the poor creature, Gorō gently picked it up and brought it into his home. He carefully bandaged its wing, fed it rice grains, and kept it warm in a little woven basket by the hearth.

As days passed, the swallow grew stronger, and soon it was able to flutter its wings again. One morning, it perched on Gorō’s shoulder and chirped joyfully. It was time to leave.

As he opened his window, the swallow hopped onto the sill, looked back at him, and then flew away into the sky.

Gorō sighed. “Be safe, little one.”

The Mysterious Gift of the Swallow

Winter came and went, and soon the cherry blossoms began to bloom. One morning, as Gorō was preparing to plant new rice seedlings, he heard a familiar chirping outside his home.

To his amazement, a flock of swallows had gathered on his rooftop, and among them was the very same swallow he had rescued. It held something in its beak a small, golden seed and dropped it into Gorō’s hands before flying away.

Curious, Gorō planted the seed in a corner of his rice paddy. Within days, a strange plant sprouted, growing faster than any rice he had ever seen. Its leaves were bright green, and its stalks shimmered like gold.

When harvest time came, Gorō found that instead of ordinary rice grains, the plant had produced golden grains, each one as precious as a pearl. Overjoyed, he used them to buy better tools, help his neighbors, and repair the village shrine.

His fortune grew, and word of the "Golden Rice of the Swallow" spread throughout the land. But Gorō never became greedy—he always shared his blessings with others, remembering that the swallow had given him this gift out of kindness.

The Jealous Neighbor's Fate

One day, Gorō’s neighbor, a greedy man named Saburō, heard about the golden rice and became envious. He thought, “If a swallow could bring fortune to Gorō, I will catch one and force it to give me golden seeds, too!”

So, Saburō trapped a swallow in a bamboo cage and broke its wing, thinking it would have no choice but to give him treasure. But instead of golden seeds, when he released it in spring, the swallow never returned.

The following year, a terrible storm destroyed Saburō’s rice fields, and he lost everything. In his greed, he had brought misfortune upon himself.

The Meaning of the Swallow’s Tale

To this day, swallows in Japan are seen as symbols of good luck and protection, especially if they choose to nest in your home or barn.

People believe that showing kindness to swallows will bring prosperity and happiness, just as the kind farmer was rewarded for his good heart. But harming them out of greed brings misfortune, just like the jealous neighbor.

So, when spring arrives, and you see swallows darting through the sky, remember:

  • They are not just birds; they are messengers of fortune.
  • They reward those who show compassion and gratitude.
  • And if one ever builds a nest in your home, welcome it with an open heart—for it may bring blessings in return.
